Insulin Types and How They Work

Insulin is a hormone that allows glucose to enter cells for energy. People with diabetes may need insulin therapy when the body doesn't produce enough.

RAPID-ACTING INSULIN:
- Examples: Lispro (Humalog), Aspart (NovoLog), Glulisine (Apidra), Fiasp
- Onset: 10-30 minutes
- Peak: 1-2 hours
- Duration: 3-5 hours
- Use: taken at mealtime to cover carbohydrates, or to correct high blood sugar

SHORT-ACTING INSULIN (Regular):
- Examples: Humulin R, Novolin R
- Onset: 30-60 minutes
- Peak: 2-4 hours
- Duration: 5-8 hours
- Use: taken 30-45 minutes before meals

INTERMEDIATE-ACTING INSULIN (NPH):
- Examples: Humulin N, Novolin N
- Onset: 1-3 hours
- Peak: 4-12 hours
- Duration: 12-18 hours
- Use: once or twice daily background insulin

LONG-ACTING INSULIN:
- Examples: Glargine (Lantus, Basaglar), Detemir (Levemir), Degludec (Tresiba)
- Onset: 1-2 hours
- No pronounced peak
- Duration: 20-24 hours (Tresiba: up to 42 hours)
- Use: once-daily background (basal) insulin

PREMIXED INSULIN:
- Combination of rapid/short-acting and intermediate-acting
- Examples: 70/30, 75/25 formulations
- Less flexibility in dosing

INSULIN DELIVERY:
- Syringes: traditional method
- Insulin pens: more convenient, prefilled cartridges
- Insulin pumps: continuous subcutaneous infusion, most flexible
- Inhaled insulin (Afrezza): rapid-acting, inhaled at meals

Storage: Keep unopened insulin in the refrigerator (36-46°F). In-use vials/pens can be kept at room temperature (<77°F) for 28-30 days depending on product.

Always consult your healthcare provider or diabetes educator for insulin dosing guidance.

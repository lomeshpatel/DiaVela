Diabetes and Foot Care

People with diabetes are at higher risk for foot problems due to nerve damage (neuropathy) and poor circulation (peripheral artery disease). Foot complications are the leading cause of non-traumatic lower limb amputations.

DAILY FOOT CARE ROUTINE:
1. Inspect feet daily — check all surfaces, between toes, for cuts, blisters, redness, swelling, or nail problems
2. Wash feet daily in warm (not hot) water — test water temperature with elbow
3. Dry thoroughly, especially between toes
4. Moisturize — apply lotion to top and bottom but NOT between toes (prevents fungal infections)
5. Never walk barefoot, even indoors
6. Trim toenails straight across, file edges — don't cut corners
7. Wear clean, dry, well-fitting socks (seamless preferred) and proper footwear

CHOOSING FOOTWEAR:
- Wear shoes that fit well with adequate toe room
- Break in new shoes gradually
- Check inside shoes for foreign objects before putting on
- Consider diabetic/therapeutic footwear if recommended by your doctor

WARNING SIGNS — SEE A DOCTOR PROMPTLY:
- Any cut, sore, or wound that doesn't heal within a few days
- Ingrown toenails or toenail infections
- Calluses or corns (don't try to remove at home)
- Redness, warmth, or swelling
- Numbness, tingling, or pain in feet/legs
- Color changes (pale, bluish, or dark discoloration)
- Any blister, even painless

DIABETIC NEUROPATHY:
- Peripheral neuropathy can cause loss of sensation — you may not feel injuries
- Symptoms: numbness, tingling, burning, or shooting pain, usually starting in toes
- Autonomic neuropathy can cause dry skin (increased injury risk)

CLINICAL FOOT EXAMS:
- Complete foot examination at every primary care visit (for those with complications)
- Annual comprehensive foot exam including monofilament test for sensation

Never use heating pads, hot water bottles, or electric blankets on your feet. Never use chemical corn/callus removers.
